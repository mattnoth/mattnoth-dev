Mike: What you said - "Oh okay. I can dig it. So like you "have to be logged in" to write a paper or take a test or something."  is correct- the first barrier to cheating is with networking/IT creating an audit trail for each student when logged in. The other additional core concept has to do with chat session transcripts. I went into the weeds there when we were texting earlier. 

The main premise is that education needs to adapt to LLMs. Right now as I understand it is a never ending circle of slop - students use AI to complete AI written tasks. LLM needs to be positioned as a research tool only-not a writing tool. Everything downstream enforces that. 

Quick glossary: 
LLM: Large Language Model, chatGPT, Claude, etc. 
Context Engineer: a Software Engineer with AI usage skill-set, specifically, the semantics and session window, the Agent's memory. I will try and use uppercase Context when referring to an Agent's Context.
Agent: In this context (no pun intended) I am referring to one session / chat.


1. My example is paper driven, specifically a paper written IN class. If remote, their computer is completely locked in and they have to be on video. 
2. If students use AI, they are required to use Sanctioned AI - when "logged in". Students  submit the transcript of any and all conversations related to the topic. 
3. The enterprise model (what school pays for) is given specific, strong direction on how to format its output to make generating prose as difficult as possible; although, still, not unbreakable. 
4. A Context Engineer further enforces rules "on the ground floor" so to speak. All student chats must go through the UI wrapped in the Context Engineer's UI. 
    Being "logged in"  adds strong boundaries, guardrails, etc. that are actually difficult for the model to break when worded correctly in their instructions; although not unbreakable. You obviously don't wanna sit around and read shitty papers, let alone shitty chat conversations with students and the bot to vaguely speculate. 
5. There would be an automated analysis of student papers ( which can be done cost-effectively ), which maybe flags 10-15% of the papers for review, at which time, every single chat session for that student (or paper, but overly technical detail) is analyzed, by a Context specialist or a highly specialized Agent (technically, a Multi-Agent Orchestration to look for things like "write me 5 lines of this/that" that are used in the paper).

Teachers do not read the papers until the process above is completed. Teachers I'm sure especially do not want to read transcripts. 

There are ways around this, obviously, like using your phone or other computer to get it to write for you, and then retyping something written while "logged out". 

Even so, the idea here is to slowly shift the process to match the current paradigm. You know, before we become completely abstracted from reality.
